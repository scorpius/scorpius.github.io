Mpeg-1 for Dummies
January 22, 2001

Bitrate
Increasing the bitrate gives higher quality and bigger file size. In other words, it is directly proportional to both the file size and quality. Example:
Your animation has 400 frames and you want to encode at 25fps. A bitrate of 1,000,000 bits/sec will produce a 2,000,000 byte file (excluding the header, packets, and other chunks of junk).
400frames/25fps = 16 seconds
1,000,000 bits/sec = 125,000 bytes/sec
16 seconds x 125,000 bytes/sec = 2,000,000 byte mpeg file (~1.9MB).

Frame rate
Decreasing the frame rate will increase the file size (inversely proportional) and will have no effect on quality. The minimum should be 24 fps, to avoid flickering.

GOP sequence (IPB)
Increasing the I frames in the GOP will increase quality and file size. Increasing P and B frames will do the opposite. I frames are like keyframes; P and B frames depend on them in order to be decoded. I frames are compressed/degraded the least and since P and B are derived from I frames, they are degraded to a greater extent.
